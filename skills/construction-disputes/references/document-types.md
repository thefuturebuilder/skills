## 1. Contract Forms

### MBA (Master Builders Association)
- **DB4**: Domestic building contract (cost-plus or lump sum). Common in Tasmania for residential work. Key features: often includes special conditions that modify standard terms; may contain clauses excluding common law termination/repudiation rights.
- **DB2**: Smaller domestic works contract
- **Commercial contracts**: Various forms for commercial building work

### HIA (Housing Industry Association)
- Residential building contracts commonly used across Australia
- Typically more owner-friendly than MBA forms

### AS2124 / AS4000
- Australian Standard general conditions of contract
- AS4000 is the more modern form, replacing AS2124
- Used primarily for commercial and government projects

### ABIC
- Australian Building Industry Contracts
- Joint initiative of the Australian Institute of Architects and Master Builders Australia

### Key Contract Elements to Extract
When reviewing any construction contract, always identify:
- **Parties**: Full legal entity names (Pty Ltd, trusts, partnerships)
- **Contract sum**: Including GST treatment
- **Construction period**: Start date, completion date, extensions of time mechanism
- **Practical completion**: Definition and process
- **Defects liability period**: Duration and notification requirements
- **Variation mechanism**: How variations are claimed, assessed, and approved
- **Payment terms**: Progress claims, timing, retention
- **Dispute resolution**: Mediation, adjudication, arbitration, or court
- **Termination provisions**: Builder's right, owner's right, consequences
- **Special conditions**: ALWAYS read these — they override standard conditions

## 2. Subcontractor and Supply Agreements

### Purchase Orders (POs)
POs are often the primary contractual document with subcontractors in residential construction. They define:
- Scope of work (critical: "supply only" vs "supply and install" vs "labour only")
- Price (lump sum or schedule of rates)
- Individual line items — each line item should be treated as a discrete obligation
- Timing requirements
- Back-charges and retention provisions

**Analysis tips:**
- Always read every line item individually — don't summarise or combine them
- Cross-reference PO line items against invoices to see what was actually claimed and paid
- Check whether work described in the PO matches what was actually performed on site
- Look for discrepancies between PO scope and what the subcontractor claims their scope was

### Subcontractor Agreements
More formal than POs, these typically include:
- Back-to-back provisions (mirroring head contract obligations)
- Insurance requirements
- Indemnities
- Retention provisions
- Dispute resolution (may differ from head contract)

### Supply Agreements
For materials supply only (no installation). Key distinction:
- If a supplier only supplies materials, they're typically not responsible for installation defects
- But if their PO includes installation line items, they may have broader responsibility
- Check invoices carefully — "SUPPLY ONLY" on an invoice doesn't override installation obligations in the PO

## 3. Financial Documents

### Progress Claims
Builder's claims for payment under the contract. Check:
- Claim number and date
- Amount claimed (ex GST and inc GST)
- Work completed vs contract schedule
- Retention held
- Any variations included

### Invoices
From subcontractors and suppliers. When analysing invoices:
- Match against the relevant PO
- Check whether the invoice description matches the PO scope
- Look for invoices that describe work differently from the PO (e.g., PO says "supply and install" but invoice says "supply only")
- Note any secondary subcontractor invoices (sub engaging their own subs)

### Variation Claims
Check each variation against:
- The contract's variation mechanism (was the process followed?)
- Whether written approval was obtained before work commenced
- The valuation methodology
- Whether the work was actually a variation or was already in the contract scope

### Payment Summaries
Running totals of what's been claimed, approved, paid, and outstanding. Useful for understanding the financial state of the project.

## 4. Permits and Approvals

### Building Permits
- Issued by the building surveyor under state Building Act
- References the approved plans and engineering
- May have conditions attached
- The permit number and date are important reference points
- Note: The filename may include words like "Endorsed" that aren't part of the official document title — always check the actual document header

### Planning Permits
- Issued by the local council
- May have conditions that affect the building design
- Planning permit conditions can conflict with what the owner or architect wants

### Certificates of Occupancy / Completion
- Issued at the end of construction
- Confirms the building complies with the permit and relevant codes
- Absence of this certificate can be significant in disputes

### Building Surveyor Correspondence
- Inspection reports and compliance assessments
- Directions to rectify non-compliant work
- These can be critical evidence in defect disputes

## 5. Plans and Drawings

### Architectural Plans
- Building Approval (BA) plans: The plans submitted to and approved by the building surveyor
- Construction plans: May differ from BA plans (watch for discrepancies)
- Revision history: Track what changed between revisions and why
- Floor plans should show dimensions — these become evidence if there are height/size disputes

### Engineering Plans
- Structural engineering (steel, concrete, timber framing)
- Civil engineering (drainage, site works)
- The engineering revision and BA set date matter for determining what was actually approved

### Specialist Plans
- Hydraulic plans (plumbing, drainage)
- Electrical plans
- Pool plans and specifications
- Landscape plans

**Analysis tips:**
- When there's a discrepancy between plans and what was built, identify which plans were the "approved" plans at the time of construction
- Note revision numbers and dates — later revisions may not have been approved
- Cross-reference specifications on plans against what the contract or POs require

## 6. Legal Documents in Disputes

### Statement of Claim (or Writ)
- The document that starts the court proceedings
- Sets out the plaintiff's case: facts relied on, legal basis, relief sought
- Each paragraph typically needs to be responded to in the Defence

### Defence (and Counterclaim)
- The defendant's response to the Statement of Claim
- May include a counterclaim (the defendant's own claims against the plaintiff)
- Paragraphs should be cross-referenced against the Statement of Claim
- Build a matrix: for each allegation, what does the Defence say? (admits/denies/does not admit/says nothing)

### Reply
- The plaintiff's response to new matters raised in the Defence
- Particularly important for responding to counterclaims

### Rule 261 Schedule (Tasmania)
- Under Division 18 of the Supreme Court Rules 2000
- A demand for the other party to particularise their claims
- The responding party must provide details and evidence supporting each claim
- Useful for forcing the other side to commit to specific positions and figures

### Interlocutory Applications
- Applications made during the proceedings (e.g., for discovery, for summary judgment)
- Discovery requests: demands for documents relevant to the case

### Calderbank Offers / Offers of Compromise
- Settlement offers made "without prejudice save as to costs"
- If the other side rejects the offer and does worse at trial, they may face adverse cost consequences
- Track all offers made and received, amounts, dates, and expiry

## 7. Expert Reports

### Defect Reports
Typically prepared by building consultants or engineers. For each defect identified:
- Nature of the defect
- Location
- Cause (workmanship, design, materials, or combination)
- Proposed rectification method
- Costing (often the most contentious part)

**Critical analysis points:**
- Does the rectification method match what the contract actually specified? (e.g., if the contract specified pebblecrete and the expert costs for tiling, that's a discrepancy)
- Does the costing include items that were already in the contract scope?
- Is the expert proposing a "betterment" (improving beyond the original contract specification)?
- Are there cheaper alternative rectification methods that would still achieve compliance?
- Does the expert have relevant qualifications for the specific defect?

### Engineering Reports
- Structural assessments
- Compliance assessments (does the work meet the approved plans and codes?)
- Conditional acceptances (the engineer accepts the work subject to certain conditions)

### Quantity Surveyor Reports
- Independent costings of rectification work
- Useful for challenging the other side's claimed costs

## 8. Correspondence Types

### Builder to Owner
- Progress updates
- Variation notifications
- Defect responses
- Payment claims
- Practical completion notices

### Owner to Builder (or their solicitor)
- Defect notifications
- Payment disputes
- Directions or instructions
- Termination or repudiation notices

### Solicitor Correspondence
- Letters between solicitors (often "without prejudice" or "without prejudice save as to costs")
- Court correspondence
- Discovery letters
- Settlement negotiations

### Regulatory Correspondence
- Building surveyor directions
- Council notices
- CBOS (Consumer, Building and Occupational Services) complaints and responses
- Insurance claims correspondence

## 9. Insurance and Warranty

### Builder's Warranty Insurance
- Required in most Australian states for residential building work above certain thresholds
- Coverage period and what's covered
- Claim conditions and exclusions

### Home Warranty / Statutory Warranties
- Vary by state (e.g., Tasmania has Building Act statutory warranties)
- Cover major defects for 6 years, other defects for 2 years (varies by jurisdiction)
- Important for limitation period calculations

### Professional Indemnity Insurance
- For consultants (architects, engineers, building surveyors)
- May be relevant if the consultant is joined as a concurrent wrongdoer

### Public Liability Insurance
- Standard builder's insurance
- May be relevant for third-party damage claims during construction
